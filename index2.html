Spain

Feliz Navidad

In Catalonia, when looking at the Christmas crib with Mary, Joseph and Jesus, another figure stands out - the Caganer. The curious figure squats there with his bare backside and unabashedly does his „big business“. In the supposed 200-year-old tradition, the man was originally hidden behind bushes or haystacks with his pants down, so that children had to search for the figure every year anew. However, the intention was always to draw attention to the essential: the newborn baby Jesus.

Sweden

God Jul

During the dark Christmas season, Swedes celebrate the atmospheric Lucia Festival, dedicated to Saint Lucia, on December 13. It is considered the counterpart of Midsummer‘s Eve, which is also celebrated annually in Sweden and other Scandinavian countries. In Christianity, December 13 is the name day of Saint Lucia, who gave the festival its name. The most important element of the celebration is the election of a Lucia, usually the eldest daughter in families. She wears a wreath of lights on her head and leads a procession, the highlight of the festival. Hot glögg, similar to mulled wine, and saffron cookies are also part of the celebration. While we Germans like to watch „Little Lord Fauntleroy“ „Home Alone“ or „Three Wishes for Cinderella“ on TV at Christmas, the Swedes watch a Donald Duck Christmas special. The show about the choleric duck in a sailor suit has been broadcast year after year since 1959.

Mexico

Feliz Navidad

Christmas is by far the most important holiday in strict Catholic Mexico and is therefore celebrated extensively. During the nine days before Christmas Eve, children from the neighborhood gather to re-enact Mary and Joseph‘s journey to Bethlehem. „Posada“ means „shelter“ and is emblematic of the stable where Jesus was born. Every evening between December 16 and 24, the children walk in small groups from house to house, symbolically asking the residents for a place to stay. Traditionally, the children are sent away in the first houses until they are welcomed by a house. In the host‘s house, the Posada evening is celebrated with a small party including games, prayers and fireworks. Highlights for the children include the piñata, a colorful cardboard figure filled with candy. To grab the sweet treats, the children must blindfoldedly beat the piñata with a stick until it breaks.

Venezuela

Felices Fiestas

In the Venezuelan capital Caracas, it has been a tradition for years to go to church on Christmas Eve on roller skates. Seriously, we didn‘t make that up. The trend has become so popular that the police now cordon off entire streets to allow the skaters to pass unhindered.

Norway

Hyggelig Jul

If you want to sweep the cookie crumbs off the floor in Norway at Christmas, you usually have a problem - all the brooms and mops in the house have disappeared off the face of the earth. According to a deep-rooted superstition of the Norwegians, on Christmas night all the witches and ghosts venture out of their hiding places to do all kinds of mischief with people‘s possessions. The witches search the houses for brooms with which they can fly wildly through the cities and cause chaos. Therefore, to keep the witches from their nightly jaunts, the Norwegians hide all the brooms. That way, they don‘t have to climb onto a neighbor‘s rooftop later and bring them down again.
